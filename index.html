Chapter One

Core Gameplay

This system is built around competent but human characters facing dangerous, often overwhelming situations. Whether investigating strange events, surviving a hostile environment, or fighting, everything runs through one resolution mechanic.

The Gameplay Loop

Every session follows the same structure. The GM describes what characters perceive. Players declare what their characters attempt. The GM determines whether the outcome is certain or requires a roll.

Resolve Outcome

When success is uncertain, the GM calls for a Trait + Skill check and sets a Target Number (TN). Roll a pool of D10s and keep a number equal to the relevant Trait. If the total of kept dice meets or exceeds the TN, the action succeeds. Failure produces complications, danger, or a partial outcome at the GM's discretion.

Game Master Note

Not every action needs a roll. If the outcome is certain, or if failure would produce only tedium rather than drama, let it happen. Rolls are for moments of genuine uncertainty where the result matters.

Character Creation

Characters begin as capable individuals with room to grow. Creation follows four steps.

Step 1 - Starting Traits

All Traits and the Spirit Ring start at 1. Choose one Trait to increase by 1. Traits and Rings have a maximum of 5, but during character creation the maximum is 3, with two exceptions: your starting Language (English) and any Trait or skill granted through pre-character development, which may reach 4.

Step 2 - Experience Points

You have 120 XP to spend during creation. All characters receive Language: English at Rank 3 at no cost. Choose 3 skills with a Level 1 cost of no more than 3 XP; these start at Rank 1 for free. One of these free slots may raise English to Rank 4 instead.

Example - Buying a Skill

John wants his character to be skilled with pistols. He spends 1 XP for Rank 1, 2 XP for Rank 2, and 4 XP for Rank 3, totaling 7 XP. To advance later to Rank 4, he pays only the cost listed for Rank 4 (8 XP). Rank 5 costs 16 XP.

Step 3 - Specializations

Choose a number of free Skill Specializations equal to your Fire Ring. Each skill may hold exactly one Specialization. Additional Specializations require the Specialized Advantage.

Step 4 - Starting Gear

Characters begin with 10 Expendable Wealth to purchase starting gear, and may also spend from Personal Wealth at GM discretion.

The Spirit Ring

Spirit has two values: Maximum Spirit (the physical trait of the ring) and Current Spirit (the mental trait). Current Spirit is spent during play and recovers through rest, Meditation, or GM reward. Maximum Spirit is the upper ceiling.

Skill Checks

The Roll at a Glance
  • Roll Trait + Skill dice (D10s)
  • Keep a number equal to your Trait
  • A 10 explodes - reroll and add
  • Total kept dice vs. TN
  • Meet or beat = success

When success is uncertain, the GM calls for a Trait + Skill check. Roll a number of D10s equal to your Trait value plus your Skill rank. Keep a number of dice equal to your Trait and total them. A die showing 10 explodes: reroll it and add the new result to that die's total.

If the final total meets or exceeds the Target Number, the action succeeds.

Unskilled Checks

Attempting a Skill check without any ranks makes you Unskilled. Roll and keep only a number of D10s equal to your Trait. The TN increases by 10, and Raises of any kind are unavailable.

Trait Checks

When no skill applies, the GM may call for a raw Trait check. Roll and keep a number of D10s equal to your Trait value.

Raises

Raises push an action further: ricocheting a shot around a corner, landing a called shot, adding force to a hit. Each Raise increases the TN by 5.

Raises can only be declared one of two ways:

  • Before the roll: Declare any number of Raises before rolling. Each adds 5 to the TN.
  • After the roll: Spend Current Spirit to make Raises after seeing the result, up to your Current Spirit rating. Each still adds 5 to the TN. If the total no longer meets the new TN, the roll fails.
Critical Rule

Only one method per roll. Raises declared before rolling cannot be combined with post-roll Raises, and vice versa.

Skills & Specializations

Skills represent trained capabilities. Higher ranks add more dice to the pool and, for certain skills, unlock Techniques. Each Skill holds exactly one Specialization, which typically grants +5 or +10, reduces a TN, or unlocks a new action.

The Fire Ring determines how many free Specializations a character receives at creation. Two characters with identical skills can play very differently depending on their Specializations - these choices define a character's niche within the party.

Chapter Two

Action & Combat

Combat is fast, lethal, and tactical. Action economy matters. Positioning matters. Spirit management matters. A single well-placed hit can change a fight. This chapter covers time, actions, damage, and survival.

Initiative & Rounds

Combat is divided into Rounds, each consisting of 10 Action Phases. Each Phase represents roughly 6 seconds. A Round begins at Phase 1 and advances phase by phase as actions resolve.

Actions per Round

The number of available actions each Round - called Action Dice - is calculated from the sum of all five Ring values. Two variants exist:

VariantFormulaFeel
Standard (Sum of all Rings) ÷ 5, round down Fewer actions; slower, grittier combat pace.
+1 Variant (recommended) 1 + (Sum of all Rings) ÷ 5, round down One guaranteed extra action; combats move faster.
Playtester Note

The +1 variant was added after playtesting showed that low-Ring characters sometimes had only 1–2 Action Dice per Round, making combat sluggish and leaving players with little to do. The extra die meaningfully speeds play without unbalancing the action economy. Use the standard formula for a harsher, more tactical game.

Rolling Initiative

At the start of each Round, roll a number of D10s equal to your Action Dice. The number showing on each die is the Phase on which you may take that action. Lower numbers act earlier.

Example

You have 3 Action Dice and roll a 2, a 6, and a 9. You may act on Phases 2, 6, and 9 this Round.

Ties in Initiative

When two characters act in the same Phase, compare their total remaining Action Dice including the current Phase. Highest total goes first. If still tied, compare Air Ring, then Fire Ring, then Spirit Ring in succession. If the tie persists, the GM may rule simultaneous resolution.

Special Actions

Held Actions

Hold up to a number of Action Dice equal to your Current Spirit, spending them on a later Phase instead of when they originally rolled. Held dice retain their original rolled value for tie-breaking and cannot carry over into the next Round.

Interrupt Actions

Spend 2 Action Dice from later Phases to perform an action on an earlier Phase. That action always resolves last in the Phase. If multiple characters use Interrupt actions in the same Phase, they resolve in the order declared.

Exception: An Interrupt used for an Active Defense resolves simultaneously with the triggering attack.

Full Defense

Before initiative is rolled at the start of a Round, declare Full Defense. Initiative rolls normally, but for the entire Round Action Dice may only be used for Active Defense, movement, or support actions such as healing, assisting, or repositioning. Attacks and offensive actions are unavailable. Characters with the Lightning Reflexes Advantage gain +5 to Active Defense rolls while in Full Defense.

Defense

Passive Defense

Passive TN is how hard a character is to hit without an active response. Two variants exist - choose one and apply it consistently.

VariantMelee TNRanged TNFeel
StandardWater Ring x 5Air Ring x 5Characters are easier to hit; combat is grittier and faster.
+5 Variant (recommended)5 + (Water Ring x 5)5 + (Air Ring x 5)Characters are harder to hit; more survivable combat.
Playtester Note

The +5 variant came from actual play. The standard formula produced passive TNs low enough that even average attackers rarely missed, making characters feel fragile. A flat +5 to both values creates more meaningful tension without changing the underlying system. Recommended for most groups, particularly new ones.

Add the Defense skill rank to whichever base TN is in use. Bonuses from cover, status effects, and other modifiers apply on top.

Active Defense

When attacked, spend an available Action Die to attempt an Active Defense. Roll Reflexes + applicable Skill vs. the TN of the attacker's roll. Meeting or beating the TN negates the attack entirely.

Tip

The Defense skill adds its rank directly to both passive Melee and Ranged TN, making it one of the most efficient investments for survivability. The Dodgy specialization doubles this bonus.

Attacking

When rolling to hit with a weapon or unarmed, roll Agility + applicable Skill. The GM may substitute a different Trait in specific cases, such as Strength for a grappling attack, but Agility is standard. The default hit location is the Chest.

Called Shots

Spend Raises to target a specific body location. The mechanical effects of hitting specific locations - disarming, reducing movement, affecting a particular ability - are adjudicated by the GM.

Raises SpentTarget Location

Off-Hand Penalty

Attacking with the non-dominant hand imposes a -2K2 penalty to the roll. The Ambidexterity Advantage negates 1K1 per level purchased.

Damage & Wounds

Damage is expressed as two numbers separated by a K - for example, 3K2. The first number is how many D10s to roll; the second is how many to keep. Total the kept dice for the damage result.

Melee Damage

Add the Strength Trait to the first number of the weapon's damage rating. Unarmed damage is (Str)K1: a number of dice equal to Strength, keeping one.

Ranged Damage

Most ranged weapons have fixed damage that increases only through Raises or special abilities. Bows with a Power rating are the exception - they allow adding some or all of Strength to the damage dice rolled.

Armor

Armor reduces incoming Flesh Wounds. Determine whether the attack is Melee or Ranged, check whether the hit location is protected, and reduce the Flesh Wounds taken by the armor's listed value. Different values apply to Melee and Ranged attacks. If the location is unprotected, no reduction applies.

Flesh Wounds

Flesh Wounds are superficial injuries with no immediate lasting effect. Whenever any Flesh Wounds are taken, make a Wound Check. If the check fails and a Dramatic Wound is gained, all current Flesh Wounds are erased.

The Wound Check

Make a Stamina Trait check. The TN equals the current Flesh Wound total. Meeting or beating the TN leaves the Flesh Wounds in place but causes no further harm. Failing inflicts 1 Dramatic Wound, plus 1 more for every 10 below the TN. Unarmed attacks use a margin of 20 instead of 10. All Flesh Wounds are erased on a failed Wound Check.

Dramatic Wounds

Dramatic Wounds represent serious injury. Track them against the Earth Ring - the thresholds below determine condition.

Dramatic Wounds TakenStatusEffect
Less than Earth RingHealthyNo penalties
Equal to Earth RingCrippledDice no longer explode; lose 1 unkept die per DW above threshold (not on Wound Checks)
Equal to Earth Ring x 2IncapacitatedCannot act; vulnerable to death blows; requires Spirit or recovery to continue
More than Earth Ring x 2-Do not record excess Dramatic Wounds

Recovering from Incapacitation

To act while Incapacitated, spend 1 Spirit Point at the start of a Combat Round to roll 1 Action Die. For that Round only, the character is not considered Incapacitated or Crippled. Medical care that reduces Dramatic Wounds below the double-Earth threshold restores normal function.

First Aid

Roll Intelligence + Medicine with no TN. The result equals the number of Flesh Wounds removed from the target. Costs 1 Action Die in combat. Each character may use First Aid only once per combat per target, unless they have the Paramedic specialization.

Surgery

Roll Intelligence + Medicine vs. a TN of 10 x the target's current Dramatic Wounds. Success removes 1 Dramatic Wound, plus 1 more for every 10 above the TN. Failure inflicts 1 additional Dramatic Wound - and kills the target if already Incapacitated. Surgery requires 4 Action Dice. Each character may only perform Surgery once per combat per target unless they have the Surgeon specialization.

Ammo & Reloading

Reloading costs 1 Action Die unless noted otherwise. Crossbow bolts take 2 Action Dice; a box of Heavy ammunition takes 4 Action Dice, and multiple characters may contribute Action Dice to assist. Clips, Energy Cells, and Boxes restore full ammo capacity. Individual rounds restore only 1 ammo capacity each.

Disarms

Melee Disarm

Roll a contested Agility + Weapon Skill vs. the opponent's Agility + Weapon Skill. Success: the opponent loses their weapon. Success by 10 or more: choose to take the weapon. Failure: the opponent retains their weapon and gains a Free Raise on their next Melee attack.

Ranged Disarm

Roll Reflexes + Weapon Skill vs. the target's Ranged TN +10. Success: the opponent is disarmed and takes half the weapon's damage roll, rounded down.

Spirit Recovery

As Current Spirit is spent, several methods allow recovery:

  • Rest: A full night's rest restores all spent Current Spirit.
  • Meditation: Using the Meditation skill for 1 hour (TN 20) recovers 2 Current Spirit, plus 1 per 10 above the TN.
  • GM Award: The GM may restore Spirit as a reward for good roleplay, notable actions, or meaningful story moments.

The GM may also award Bonus Spirit Dice - temporary dice that cannot raise Current Spirit but can each be spent to add 1K1 to a single roll. Unused Bonus Spirit Dice at session's end convert to 1 XP each.

End-of-Session Spirit XP - Two Variants

Spirit Points can reward players at the end of each session. Two approaches produce meaningfully different effects on player behavior:

VariantRuleEffect on Play
Standard Unused Spirit Points at session's end convert to XP; spent points are lost. Players tend to hoard Spirit to maximize XP, avoiding spending it even when doing so would be tactically sound.
Full Reward (recommended) At session's end, players gain XP equal to their total Spirit Points - both used and unused. Spending Spirit carries no XP penalty. Players spend Spirit freely, producing more dynamic and dramatic moments during play.
Playtester Note

The standard rule created an unintended problem: players consistently refused to spend Spirit because they wanted the bonus XP at session's end. The Full Reward variant removes this tension entirely - players spend Spirit when it matters and still receive the full reward. Use the standard rule if you want players to be more deliberate and conservative with Spirit.

Chapter Three

Not every conflict is resolved through violence. This chapter covers how characters navigate society, spend money, communicate across language barriers, and contend with environmental hazards.

Wealth

Wealth is measured abstractly on a 1–10 scale. Each character has a Minimum Wealth and a Current Wealth. If Current Wealth falls below Minimum Wealth, it recovers by 1 per in-game month.

Making Purchases

Your Wealth vs. Item CostResult
Your rating is higher than the item's costPurchase freely. GM discretion on quantity.
Your rating equals the item's costCurrent Wealth is reduced by 1 (as long as it won't reach zero).
Your rating is lower than the item's costCurrent Wealth is reduced by 1 + 1 for each Wealth level the item exceeds your rating (as long as it won't reach zero).
Example

Freddy has a Current Wealth of 4 and wants to buy a Plasma Gun with a Wealth cost of 6. The gun costs 2 Wealth levels above his rating, so Current Wealth drops by 3 (1 + 2), bringing him to 1. He can afford it - barely.

Expendable Wealth

Expendable Wealth is temporary currency consumed on a 1-for-1 basis when purchasing items or services. It can be used regardless of Current Wealth rating and can substitute for or supplement Current Wealth when buying above rating.

Example

Freddy has Current Wealth 4 and 2 Expendable Wealth. The Plasma Gun costs 6 Wealth. Normally this would cost 3 Current Wealth, but Freddy spends 2 Expendable Wealth to offset 2 of that cost, reducing the Current Wealth drain to 1.

Social Interaction

When characters attempt to influence, deceive, intimidate, or negotiate with NPCs, the GM calls for a relevant Skill check. Common social skills include Fast Talk, Seduction, Interrogate, and Acting.

Social interactions are not pass/fail. A wide-margin success might produce a loyal contact, a significant price reduction, or durable information. A failure might create suspicion, hostility, or a closed door. Relevant modifiers:

  • Appearance Advantages (+1 or +2 Unkept dice) apply to most social rolls.
  • The Gregarious Advantage adds +1 Kept die to all social rolls.
  • The Famous Advantage adds 1K1 to all social rolls where fame is relevant.
  • Attempting to deceive or intimidate may trigger an opposed roll from the target (Perception + Intuition, for example).
GM Guidance

If a player describes their character's approach with detail and clear intent, consider granting a Free Raise before the dice are rolled. Social skills complement roleplay - they don't replace it.

Exploration & Hazards

Outside of combat, characters frequently face environmental challenges: scaling a building, surviving a storm, navigating a burning structure. The GM sets a TN appropriate to the difficulty. Commonly involved skills:

  • Athletics - physical exertion, swimming, climbing, running
  • Survival - navigating and enduring hostile environments
  • Stealth - moving unseen and unheard
  • Investigation - perceiving physical details in a scene
  • Tracking - following a quarry's trail or concealing your own

Hazards that deal damage - fire, falls, extreme cold - typically deal a fixed dice pool of Flesh Wounds and may trigger Wound Checks normally. The GM determines appropriate damage based on severity.

Chapter Four

Advancement

Characters grow through experience. Between sessions - or at moments of downtime the GM designates - players spend earned XP to raise Traits, improve Skills, and acquire Advantages.

Earning & Spending XP

The GM awards XP at the end of sessions based on participation, roleplay, story progress, and any other criteria the table agrees on. XP expenditure outside of character creation follows the costs listed in Chapter 1.

Tip for Game Masters

A useful benchmark is 3–5 XP per session for active participation, with bonuses of 1–3 XP for strong roleplay, problem-solving, or reaching a significant story milestone. Consistent awards keep advancement rewarding without outpacing the challenge curve.

Advancement Restrictions

Advantages marked with an asterisk (*) can only be purchased at character creation unless a specific in-game event justifies the acquisition. These represent innate or foundational qualities that cannot be trained into. Confirm with the GM before spending XP on any asterisked Advantage after creation.

Chapter Five

Game Master Toolkit

This chapter is for the person running the game. It covers encounter design, NPC construction, session structure, and how to guide the story while letting players shape it.

Setting Target Numbers

The Target Number is the most powerful dial available to the GM. The following benchmarks provide guidance:

TNDifficultyWhen to Use
5TrivialTasks requiring almost no effort for a competent person.
10EasyRoutine tasks a trained person handles without stress.
15ModerateTasks that require attention and skill. A solid default for most mid-session checks.
20ChallengingTasks that push an average skilled person noticeably.
25HardTasks requiring genuine expertise or exceptional effort.
30Very HardTasks at the edge of what an expert can reliably do.
35+LegendaryTasks that define careers.
Playtested GM Principle

Default to TN 15. Determine difficulty based on the fiction, not on how skilled the characters are. Set the TN for the task and let the dice and character builds determine the outcome. This keeps the world consistent and rewards investment in skills without making everything trivially easy.

Rewarding Big Rolls

If a player rolls well above the TN without spending Raises, consider a small bonus - an extra piece of information, a detail that seeds a future plot point, or a moment of narrative flair. Exceptional rolls should mean something beyond a binary pass.

Healing & Recovery

Outside of combat, characters need time to recover. Default healing pace:

  • Flesh Wounds heal fully after extended rest. A full night's sleep works, but significant downtime, a calm meal, or a few hours off their feet is usually enough.
  • Dramatic Wounds heal at roughly 1 per day with proper rest. Under a physician with adequate equipment, this may increase to 2 or more per day.
Optional - Medical Care Roll

If a character is under a physician's care, the attending character may roll Intelligence + Medicine each day. TN 15 for a decent field setup; TN 20–25 for rough conditions. A successful roll may increase healing by 1 additional Dramatic Wound that day, or grant other appropriate benefits at GM discretion.

Encounter Design

Combat is unforgiving. A single Dramatic Wound can change a fight, and Incapacitation is always one bad Wound Check away. When designing encounters:

  • Action economy matters most. More enemies with fewer Action Dice can overwhelm a party faster than one powerful opponent. Spread the threat.
  • Use the environment. Cover, lighting, elevation, and chokepoints create tactical decisions without additional rules.
  • Give enemies goals. Enemies trying to escape, capture someone, or destroy something create urgency beyond a straightforward fight.
  • Use status effects deliberately. A Paralyzed or Prone character is an opportunity for both sides. Make sure players feel the difference.

NPC Design

Most NPCs don't need a full character sheet. For minor NPCs, use the following:

NPC TierSuggested StatsNotes
MinionTraits 1–2, Skills 1–2, 2–4 Dramatic WoundsDrops quickly in small groups. One or two good hits.
VeteranTraits 2–3, Skills 2–4, 4–6 Dramatic WoundsA genuine threat one-on-one. May have one or two Advantages.
EliteTraits 3–4, Skills 3–5, 6–8 Dramatic WoundsA significant combat challenge. Multiple Advantages; distinct fighting style.
Boss / Major NPCTraits 3–5, Skills 4–5, Earth Ring 4–5Full character build. Unique Advantages or abilities. Memorable tactics.

Spirit as a Narrative Tool

Spirit is the system's representation of a character's will, determination, and dramatic momentum - not just a mechanical resource. Award Bonus Spirit Dice at moments of high drama: a rousing speech, a hard-earned victory, a sacrifice. These moments make Spirit feel earned rather than transactional.

Moments where Spirit reaches its limit - a character Incapacitated, deciding whether to spend the last point to keep fighting - are the system's version of a climactic scene. Build toward them.

Story & Session Guidance

First Rule

Have fun. That goes double for your players. If the rules are getting in the way, circumvent them in whatever manner works for the table. The rules serve the story.

This system works best when players feel competent but not invincible. Characters with focused builds shine in their areas and struggle meaningfully in others. Design sessions that let each character's specializations matter at least once.

  • Prepare situations, not scripts. Decide what NPCs want and what they'll do. Player choices determine how scenes play out.
  • Let failure drive drama. A failed Wound Check that drops someone to Crippled is not a setback - it's a scene. Play it up.
  • Reward creativity. If a player describes something inventive, let them roll for it. The Raise system means ambition is always possible; it just carries risk.
  • Rest and recovery are pacing tools. Controlling when players can safely recover Spirit and wounds controls the tension curve of the story.

Item Durability & Hardness

All items have two stats. Durability is the amount of Flesh Wounds the item can absorb before breaking - once it reaches zero, further damage passes through. Hardness functions like armor for the item, reducing incoming Flesh Wound damage by the Hardness rating before applying it to Durability.
